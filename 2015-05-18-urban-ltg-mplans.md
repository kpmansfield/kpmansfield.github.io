title: A Template for Urban Lighting Master Plans
---

With our cities awash with lighting it is perhaps a faint hope that lighting can be planned—but that is precisely the purpose of an <em>urban lighting master plan</em>. In a [paper](http://ijse.cgpublisher.com/product/pub.272/prod.82) recently published in <em>The International Journal of Sustainability</em>, Diana Del-Negro and I were concerned to improve urban lighting masterpans as tools to minimise the environmental impact of artificial (synthetic) lighting in  our cities. Artificial lighting impacts on the environment by using large amounts of energy and requires the manufacture, use and disposal of lighting equipment. The uncontrolled use of artificial lighting frequently produces <em>light pollution</em> which can have pernicious effects on humans, <em>flora</em> and <em>fauna</em>.

We wanted to identify the strengths and limitations of different lighting masterplans in distinct countries and continents, to identify their strengths and limitations and to propose the development of common environmental and sustainable guidelines.

We discussed how the life cycle of lighting equipment has an environmental impact and at each of the stages we identified energy use and the resulting production of emissions (into air and water) and waste (both hazardous and non-hazardous).

Light pollution from urban lighting is manifested in three ways:—

*	<em>Sky glow</em>—reducing contrast in the night sky.

*	<em>Glare</em>—unwanted glare from street and other outdoor light sources.

*	<em>Light trespass</em>—unwanted light from adjoining properties, activities and installations.

Light pollution can be unwelcome for humans but also for <em>flora</em>, affecting flowering and the extent and direction of growth. In the case of <em>fauna</em>, birds, for example, suffer loss of cues to orientation and naviagation along migration routes and invertebrate feeding and breeding behaviour may be disrupted.

Ideally, an urban lighting masterplan should **fully** address and seek to minimise the environmental impacts of lighting. In an analysis of many international urban lighting masterplans*, curated by Diana, all of them addressed light pollution and energy efficient problems, but only three concerned themselves with <em>flora</em> and <em>fauna</em> protection and lighting maintenance issues. Only one referred to the proper disposal of redundant lamps and lighting equipment. With respect to light pollution only three cities considered <em>sky glow</em> and <em>glare and</em> light trespass; other cities considered one issue only. The masterplans did not seem to address the rich variety of <em>fauna</em> that exist in a city with strategies developed piecemeal for perhaps birds or flying insects or in some cases with no consideration at all.

We proposed therefore an urban lighting masterplan template leading to a specific set of recommendations:—

*	The masterplan should specify appropriate procurement procedures to ensure the minimisation of energy use, waste production and emissions to air and water.

*	To minimise the environmental impact of lighting on humans we suggested abolishing inefficient lighting equipment to reduce <em>sky glow</em>, using appropriate optical control to reduce <em>glare</em> and careful siting and maintenance of lighting equipment to avoid <em>light trespass</em>.

*Note:—The urban lighting masterplans considered in the paper were from Lyon, Vienna, Toronto, Gwangju and Sydney. Our thanks to the city authorities concerned.
