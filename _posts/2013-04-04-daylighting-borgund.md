title: The Daylighting of the Stave Church of Borgund, Norway
---

I have always been intrigued by descriptions in the art history literature of the atmospheric effects of daylighting in sacred buildings. For example, Norberg-Schulz in his book <em>Nightlands: Nordic Building</em> describes the interior of a stave church thus

> the weak light is split by roof work and sinks like a dim atmosphere into the room.

![](/images/borgundexterior.jpg)

<span style="color:gray; font-style:italic;">The stave church of Borgund, Norway.</span>

<span style="color:gray;">(www.wondermondo.com/Norway.htm [CC BY-SA 2.0])</span>

In a recent desktop study of the daylighting of the stave church of Borgund in Norway, I claim that the true spirit of Nordic lighting is to be found in the original <em>medieval</em> church and attempt to characterise the quality of daylighting that was achieved. The work was presented at the <em>Nordic Lighting Conference</em> 2012 and can be found [here](http://discovery.ucl.ac.uk/1388907/).


Towards the end of the paper I consider how <em>modes of appearance</em> concepts may assist interpretation of the field of view—concepts which I consider undervalued in assessment of daylighting quality in interiors.
