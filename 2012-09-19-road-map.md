In the Sep/Oct issue of the <em>SLL</em> Newsletter, Peter Boyce has suggested

> that the lighting industry should identify its top 10 questions, to be addressed by research within the next decade, and set out a commitment to funding what it takes to get the answers. This could help researchers and educators of lighting to stay focused on the important issues…

This chimes in very nicely with the famous essay by Richard Hamming—<em>You and Your Research</em>—where he states that great scientists "have something between 10 and 20  important problems for which they are looking for an attack". The key word here is <em><span style="color:rgb(190,80,30);">attack</span></em>—there needs to be a possible approach, a line of thrust, a push to overcome an important problem. He continues:

> If you do not work on an important problem, it's unlikely you'll do important work.

So what are the <em>important</em> problems in lighting research? And significantly, what is a possible line of attack?
